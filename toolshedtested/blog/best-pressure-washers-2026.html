PSI, GPM, and CU: What the Numbers Actually Mean

Every pressure washer listing throws three numbers at you. Here is what they mean in practice.

PSI (pounds per square inch) measures water pressure. Higher PSI cuts through tougher grime and is more effective on hard surfaces like concrete and brick. However, too much PSI on soft surfaces like wood, painted siding, or your car's clearcoat causes damage. Most home tasks need 1,500 to 2,500 PSI.

GPM (gallons per minute) measures water flow rate. Higher GPM means more water hits the surface each second, which speeds up rinsing and overall cleaning. Two pressure washers with the same PSI will clean at different speeds if their GPM differs. For home use, 1.2 to 2.0 GPM is the practical range.

CU (cleaning units) is PSI multiplied by GPM, giving you a single performance number. A 2,000 PSI / 1.5 GPM unit produces 3,000 CU. A 1,700 PSI / 1.8 GPM unit produces 3,060 CU. The second unit actually cleans faster despite lower PSI, because the higher flow rate compensates. Use CU to compare units more fairly than PSI alone.

One important note on ratings: Manufacturers often list "max PSI" which is measured at zero flow (no nozzle), not at operating pressure. The PWMA-rated PSI (measured at full flow) is the realistic number. We use PWMA-rated figures in this review where available.

Nozzle Color Guide: Which Tip to Use When

Every pressure washer comes with a set of quick-connect nozzles color-coded by spray angle. Using the wrong nozzle damages surfaces or wastes time. Here is the guide:

  • Red (0-degree): A pinpoint jet of maximum pressure. Effective for blasting a single stubborn stain from concrete. Never use on wood, vehicles, paint, or glass. Dangerous if pointed at skin.
  • Yellow (15-degree): A narrow fan suitable for heavy-duty concrete and brick cleaning. Use carefully on wood; it can raise grain or etch softwood at close range. Maintain 12 to 18 inches of distance.
  • Green (25-degree): The all-purpose nozzle. Handles most driveway, deck, and siding tasks. This is what you use 80% of the time.
  • White (40-degree): Wide fan, lower impact. Use on vehicles, patio furniture, painted surfaces, and windows. Safe for most materials.
  • Black (soap): Low pressure designed to apply detergent without rinsing it off immediately. Always apply soap with this nozzle, then switch to green or yellow to rinse.

What to Look for When Buying a Pressure Washer

PSI and GPM (Use Both Numbers)

Do not buy based on PSI alone. Multiply PSI by GPM to get cleaning units (CU), which is the more accurate performance measure. A 2,000 PSI / 1.5 GPM unit (3,000 CU) cleans faster than a 2,400 PSI / 1.1 GPM unit (2,640 CU) despite the lower peak pressure.

Motor Type

Universal brush motors are found in most budget electric washers. They work fine for occasional use but wear out faster than induction motors. If you plan to use your washer regularly throughout the season, the Westinghouse-style induction motor is worth the extra cost. For two to four uses per year, a universal motor is perfectly adequate.

Total Stop System (TSS)

This feature, sometimes called auto-stop, cuts the motor when you release the trigger. It prevents the pump from running unloaded (which generates heat and wear) and extends pump life significantly. In 2026, TSS is standard on all reputable electric pressure washers. If you find a unit without it, look elsewhere.

Hose Length

The included hose length determines how far you can work from the unit. A 20-foot hose is the budget standard. A 25 to 30-foot hose significantly reduces how often you need to move the machine. For vehicles, a longer hose lets you walk completely around the car without stopping. For large driveways, it reduces the number of times you reposition.

Onboard Detergent Storage

Built-in detergent tanks are more convenient than inline injectors or bucket-dipping setups. The Sun Joe's dual-tank system is the most practical implementation in this price range. Single tanks work fine for most users who use one detergent type per session.

Pro Tip: Clean Your Driveway Now

Spring is the ideal time to pressure wash your driveway. Winter ice melt, road salt, and grit work into concrete over the winter and continue to degrade the surface if left there. A thorough spring pressure washing removes these contaminants before they cause lasting pitting and staining. Apply a concrete sealer after washing for added protection. This single annual task significantly extends the life of your driveway.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many PSI do I need for a home pressure washer?

For most home tasks, 1,500 to 2,000 PSI handles cars, patio furniture, and light deck cleaning. For driveways and concrete, 2,000 to 2,800 PSI is more effective. Anything over 3,000 PSI risks damaging wood, vehicle paint, and vinyl siding if held too close.

What is GPM and why does it matter?

GPM stands for gallons per minute and measures water flow rate. Higher GPM speeds up rinsing and improves overall cleaning effectiveness. For home use, 1.2 to 2.0 GPM is the practical range. Always multiply PSI by GPM to get a realistic comparison between models.

Electric vs. gas pressure washer: which is better for home use?

Electric wins for home use in almost every scenario. They start instantly, require no gas or oil, run quieter, and need very little maintenance. Gas models produce more PSI and GPM for commercial use, but a quality electric washer handles every typical home cleaning task effectively at a lower cost and with less hassle.

Can I use a pressure washer on my car?

Yes, with the right technique. Use the 40-degree wide-angle nozzle, keep at least 12 to 18 inches from the surface, and never aim directly at rubber seals, wiper blades, or wheel center caps. Use a dedicated car wash soap applied with the soap nozzle, then rinse with the 40-degree tip.

How do I store a pressure washer for winter?

Flush all water from the pump before storage. Run pump protector antifreeze solution through the system if you are in a climate where the storage area can freeze, disconnect all hoses, and store indoors above freezing. Water left in the pump during a freeze is the most common cause of pressure washer failure and is not covered by most warranties.
