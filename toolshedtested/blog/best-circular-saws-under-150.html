Why Budget Saws Are Good Enough

There is a persistent myth in the tool world that you need to spend big to get quality. When it comes to circular saws, that myth deserves to be retired. Here is why.

Motor Technology Has Plateaued at This Level

Most corded circular saws in the $60 to $150 range run 15-amp motors producing between 5,000 and 5,800 RPM. That is more than enough power to rip through 3/4-inch plywood, crosscut dimensional lumber, and handle the vast majority of construction and renovation tasks. The difference between a $150 saw and a $350 saw is rarely about raw cutting power. It is about fit-and-finish, dust management, guide systems, and jobsite durability over years of professional daily use.

Blade Quality Matters More Than the Saw

I tested every saw on this list with both the included blade and a Diablo D0724A 24-tooth framing blade. In every single case, swapping to a quality aftermarket blade improved cut quality dramatically. A $12 blade upgrade can make a $65 saw cut as cleanly as a $200 saw running its stock blade. If you are buying a budget circular saw, plan to spend an extra $10 to $15 on a good blade and you will be amazed at the results.

What You Actually Sacrifice

Let me be honest about what budget saws give up. You will typically see more vibration at full speed, less precise bevel detents, stamped steel rather than magnesium components (which adds weight), and simpler dust management. None of these are dealbreakers for the vast majority of users, but they are worth knowing about. If you are making hundreds of cuts per day on a commercial jobsite, yes, the premium saws earn their keep. For everyone else, these budget picks are genuinely good enough.

What to Look For in a Budget Circular Saw

Choosing the right circular saw involves balancing several factors. Here are the key considerations, ranked by importance.

Blade Quality and Size

The 7-1/4 inch blade is the standard for full-size circular saws, and for good reason. It provides a cutting depth of roughly 2-1/2 inches at 90 degrees, which is enough to cut through any dimensional lumber up to a 2x10 in a single pass. More importantly, 7-1/4 inch blades are widely available and affordable, so you will always have options when it is time to replace or upgrade.

As I mentioned earlier, the included blade on most budget saws is adequate but not exceptional. Budget an extra $12 to $18 for a quality 24-tooth blade for framing and general cuts, and a 40-tooth or 60-tooth blade for when you need cleaner crosscuts in finished materials. This simple investment will transform your saw's performance.

Power and Speed

All the corded saws in this roundup run 15-amp motors, which is the standard for full-size circular saws. The key differentiator is RPM. Higher RPM generally means faster, cleaner cuts because each tooth takes a smaller bite. The Makita and Milwaukee at 5,800 RPM had a noticeable edge over the DeWalt at 5,200 RPM when cutting thick hardwoods, but for softwood framing and sheet goods, any of these saws have more than enough power.

Bevel Capacity and Accuracy

Every saw on this list can bevel to at least 50 degrees, which covers the vast majority of angle-cutting needs. The DeWalt leads at 57 degrees, followed by the Makita and Ryobi at 56 degrees. More important than maximum bevel capacity is the accuracy and repeatability of the bevel detents. Look for positive stops at 0, 22.5, and 45 degrees that you can set by feel without needing to verify with a square every time. In our testing, the Makita and DeWalt had the most precise bevel detents.

Ergonomics and Weight

Weight ranges from 7.5 pounds (Ryobi, bare tool) to 10.4 pounds (Milwaukee). If you are making a handful of cuts, weight barely matters. If you are spending a full day cutting, every ounce counts. Beyond weight, pay attention to handle comfort and trigger placement. The Milwaukee's adjustable Tilt-Lok handle is unique and genuinely useful. The DeWalt's compact design and low weight make it the most maneuverable corded option.

Pro Tip: Always Use a Guide

No matter how steady your hands are, a straightedge guide will produce better results than freehand cutting. You can buy a dedicated saw guide for $20 to $40, or simply clamp a factory-edge piece of plywood to your workpiece. The time you spend setting up a guide is always repaid with cleaner, more accurate cuts. This is especially true with budget saws, where the base plate alignment might not be as precise as premium models.

How We Tested

Every saw in this roundup went through the same standardized testing protocol to ensure fair comparisons.

Test Materials

  • Crosscuts: 2x4 kiln-dried pine, 2x4 pressure-treated southern yellow pine, and 2x10 Douglas fir
  • Rip cuts: 3/4-inch birch plywood, 3/4-inch OSB, and 3/4-inch MDF
  • Bevel cuts: 2x6 pine at 22.5 and 45 degrees

What We Measured

  • Cut accuracy: Deviation from a straight line over 4-foot and 8-foot cuts, measured with a precision straightedge and feeler gauges
  • Tearout: Visual inspection and measurement of chip-out on both entry and exit sides
  • Speed: Time to complete standardized cuts in identical materials
  • Vibration: Subjective rating on a 1-10 scale by three testers, plus accelerometer measurements
  • Bevel accuracy: Measured with a digital protractor at 22.5, 45, and maximum bevel settings
  • Noise: Measured at operator ear height with a calibrated SPL meter during rip cuts in plywood

Each saw was tested with both its included blade and the same Diablo D0724A aftermarket blade, so we could evaluate both the complete package and the saw's inherent capabilities. All tests were performed on the same day in a temperature-controlled workshop at 65 degrees Fahrenheit, using materials from the same batch to eliminate variables.

Pro Tip: Break In Your Saw

Budget circular saws often feel stiff and rough out of the box. Make 20 to 30 cuts in scrap lumber before judging the saw's performance. The motor bearings, blade guard mechanism, and depth/bevel adjustments all benefit from a break-in period. I noticed every saw on this list ran smoother and quieter after the first hour of use compared to right out of the box.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best circular saw under $150 for beginners?

The SKILSAW 5280-01 is the best choice for beginners. It is affordable at around $65, lightweight, simple to operate, and comes with a usable blade. The straightforward design means there are fewer adjustments to learn, and the 15-amp motor provides plenty of power for any beginner project. Start with the SKILSAW, learn good saw techniques, and upgrade later if you need more features.

Is a corded or cordless circular saw better?

For most users, a corded circular saw is the better choice. Corded saws deliver consistent, uninterrupted power and cost less than their cordless counterparts. The Ryobi P508 is a solid cordless option if you already own Ryobi batteries, but its lower RPM and limited battery life mean it cannot match corded saws for sustained heavy cutting. If portability is your top priority, go cordless. For everything else, corded wins.

Do I need an electric brake on my circular saw?

An electric brake is a significant safety feature that stops the blade within 1 to 2 seconds of releasing the trigger. Without one, the blade can take 10 to 15 seconds to coast to a stop. While not strictly necessary if you practice good safety habits, an electric brake adds a meaningful margin of safety, especially in situations where you might need to set the saw down quickly. The Makita, DeWalt, and Milwaukee in this roundup all include electric brakes.

Can a circular saw replace a miter saw?

A circular saw can make any cut a miter saw can, but not as quickly or easily. For repetitive crosscuts at precise angles, such as cutting deck boards or framing studs to length, a miter saw is dramatically faster. However, a circular saw with a good guide can make accurate miter and bevel cuts, and it is far more versatile overall. If you can only own one saw, make it a circular saw.

How long do circular saw blades last?

A quality carbide-tipped blade typically lasts 500 to 1,000 cuts in clean softwood before it needs sharpening. Cutting plywood, MDF, or treated lumber wears blades faster due to the adhesives and chemicals in the material. You will know it is time to replace or sharpen when you notice increased tearout, the saw bogging down more than usual, or burn marks on the wood. A good blade can be resharpened 3 to 5 times before it needs to be replaced entirely.
