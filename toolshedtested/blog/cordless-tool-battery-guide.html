The Complete Guide to Cordless Tool Batteries: 18V vs 20V, Ah Ratings Explained

If you have ever stood in the battery aisle at Home Depot staring at a wall of lithium-ion packs wondering why one says "20V MAX" and another says "18V" while they look almost identical, you are not alone. Cordless tool batteries are the engine behind every modern power tool, and understanding them can save you hundreds of dollars and years of frustration.

After testing over 40 battery packs across four major platforms, I have put together this guide to cut through the marketing speak and give you the plain-English truth about cordless tool batteries. Whether you are building out your first cordless tool collection or trying to decide between platforms, this guide will give you the knowledge to make a smart decision.

Quick Answer

18V and 20V MAX batteries deliver the same nominal voltage (18V). "20V MAX" is a marketing term that measures peak voltage under no load. For most homeowners, a 4.0Ah or 5.0Ah lithium-ion battery provides the best balance of runtime and weight. The best overall platform depends on your budget: Ryobi ONE+ for value, DeWalt 20V MAX or Milwaukee M18 for performance, and Makita 18V LXT for the best battery technology.

18V vs 20V MAX: The Marketing Truth

This is the single biggest source of confusion in the cordless tool world, so let me clear it up right now: 18V and 20V MAX are the same voltage. That is not an oversimplification. It is an electrical fact.

A lithium-ion battery cell has a nominal voltage of 3.6V (the average voltage during discharge) and a maximum voltage of 4.0V (when fully charged and under no load). When you stack five cells in series, as all major 18V/20V tool batteries do, you get:

  • Nominal voltage: 5 cells x 3.6V = 18.0V
  • Maximum voltage: 5 cells x 4.0V = 20.0V

DeWalt, Craftsman, Porter-Cable, and several other brands use the "20V MAX" designation, measuring peak voltage. Makita, Bosch, and (until recently) Milwaukee use "18V," measuring nominal voltage. The batteries themselves produce the same power output. It is purely a labeling difference.

Why Does This Matter?

It matters because it prevents you from falling for marketing tricks. When you see a tool advertised as "20V MAX" and another as "18V," your brain naturally assumes the 20V tool is more powerful. Manufacturers know this, and they chose the 20V MAX designation specifically to create that impression. Now that you know the truth, you can compare tools based on what actually matters: motor design, battery capacity (Ah), and build quality.

Pro Tip

When comparing tools across brands, ignore the voltage marketing entirely. Compare the Ah rating of the battery, the watt-hours (Wh = V x Ah), and the torque or speed specs of the tool itself. A Makita 18V tool with a 5.0Ah battery delivers the same energy (90Wh) as a DeWalt 20V MAX tool with a 5.0Ah battery.

What About 40V, 60V, and 80V Tools?

Higher voltage platforms do exist and represent genuinely different (more powerful) systems. These use more cells in series to achieve higher voltages. For example, DeWalt's 60V MAX FLEXVOLT uses 15 cells in series for a nominal 54V (max 60V). Milwaukee's MX FUEL is a true 72V platform. These high-voltage systems are designed for heavy-duty applications like concrete cutting, large miter saws, and backpack blowers that need more raw power than an 18V system can provide.

Some systems, like DeWalt's FLEXVOLT, are backward compatible, meaning a 60V MAX battery can be used in 20V MAX tools by switching from series to parallel cell configurations internally. This is genuinely clever engineering and a real advantage if you use both heavy-duty and standard tools.

What Ah (Amp-Hour) Actually Means

If voltage is the "speed" of electricity, amp-hours (Ah) is the "fuel tank." The Ah rating tells you how much energy a battery can store and deliver before it is depleted. Here is the simple explanation:

A 2.0Ah battery can deliver 2 amps for 1 hour, or 1 amp for 2 hours, or 4 amps for 30 minutes. The total energy is the same; you are just using it at different rates.

Common Ah Ratings and What They Mean in Practice

  • 1.5Ah Compact: Weighs 10-14 oz. Good for light-duty tasks like driving screws or drilling small holes. Runs a drill for roughly 30-45 minutes of intermittent use. Charges in about 30 minutes.
  • 2.0Ah Standard: Weighs 12-16 oz. The most common battery included with starter kits. Good for general-purpose work. Runs a drill for roughly 45-70 minutes. Charges in about 40 minutes.
  • 4.0Ah Extended: Weighs 18-22 oz. The sweet spot for most users. Provides excellent runtime without excessive weight. Runs a drill for 90-120 minutes. Charges in about 60 minutes.
  • 5.0Ah High Capacity: Weighs 20-26 oz. Great for power-hungry tools like circular saws and grinders. Runs a circular saw for 150-200 cuts in 2x4 lumber. Charges in about 75 minutes.
  • 6.0Ah-9.0Ah Premium: Weighs 24-32 oz. Designed for sustained heavy-duty use. Best for outdoor power equipment and continuous cutting tasks. Can feel front-heavy on compact tools.
  • 12.0Ah-15.0Ah Monster: Weighs 32-44 oz. Maximum runtime for professional all-day use. Too heavy for overhead work but great for tools that sit on a surface (miter saws, table saws).

Pro Tip

The "best" Ah rating depends on the tool. For drills and impact drivers you hold all day, a 2.0-4.0Ah compact battery keeps things light and maneuverable. For circular saws, reciprocating saws, and grinders, go with 5.0Ah or higher so you do not run out of juice mid-cut. Keep a compact and a high-capacity battery in your rotation for maximum versatility.

How Ah Relates to Watt-Hours

The truest measure of battery energy is watt-hours (Wh), calculated as voltage multiplied by amp-hours. This lets you compare batteries across different voltage platforms:

  • 18V x 5.0Ah = 90Wh
  • 18V x 4.0Ah = 72Wh
  • 54V (FLEXVOLT) x 6.0Ah = 324Wh

More watt-hours means more total energy stored in the battery. When comparing batteries, watt-hours gives you the most honest apples-to-apples measurement.

Battery Chemistry: Li-Ion vs NiCd

Modern cordless tools use lithium-ion (Li-Ion) batteries almost exclusively, but understanding the older nickel-cadmium (NiCd) technology helps explain why Li-Ion is so much better and why some old habits (like fully draining batteries before charging) are actually harmful today.

Nickel-Cadmium (NiCd): The Old Guard

NiCd batteries dominated the cordless tool world from the 1960s through the early 2000s. They are heavy, have lower energy density, and suffer from the "memory effect," where repeatedly charging a partially-drained battery causes it to "remember" a reduced capacity. NiCd batteries also self-discharge at about 15-20% per month, meaning a battery left on the shelf for a few months could be dead when you need it.

The one advantage of NiCd was cost and durability in extreme cold, but even that advantage has largely disappeared as Li-Ion technology has matured.

Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion): The Modern Standard

Lithium-ion batteries are lighter (30-50% less weight for the same capacity), have no memory effect, self-discharge at only 1-2% per month, and deliver consistent power throughout the discharge cycle rather than gradually fading. They also have a much longer lifespan, typically lasting 800-1,000+ charge cycles compared to 300-500 for NiCd.

The downside is that Li-Ion batteries are more sensitive to extreme temperatures and overcharging, which is why every modern Li-Ion battery pack includes a Battery Management System (BMS) chip that monitors cell voltage, temperature, and current draw to protect the cells from damage.

What About Lithium Iron Phosphate (LiFePO4)?

Some manufacturers are beginning to experiment with LiFePO4 chemistry, which offers even longer cycle life (2,000+ cycles) and better thermal stability at the cost of slightly lower energy density. As of early 2026, no major tool brand has released a LiFePO4 battery for their standard platform, but it is a technology worth watching for future developments.

How to Maximize Battery Life

A lithium-ion battery that is well cared for can last 3-5 years and deliver 800+ charge cycles. A neglected battery might fail within a year. Here are the evidence-based best practices that will maximize the lifespan and performance of your cordless tool batteries.

1. Store at 40-60% Charge

If you are not going to use a battery for more than a month, store it at roughly half charge. Storing Li-Ion batteries at full charge or completely empty accelerates chemical degradation of the cells. Most chargers do not have a "storage mode," so simply use the battery briefly after charging before putting it on the shelf.

2. Avoid Extreme Temperatures

Lithium-ion cells degrade fastest at high temperatures. Never leave batteries in a hot car (temperatures inside a parked car can exceed 140 degrees Fahrenheit on a summer day), in direct sunlight, or near heat sources. The ideal storage temperature is 50-77 degrees Fahrenheit. Cold temperatures (below 32 degrees Fahrenheit) temporarily reduce performance but do not cause permanent damage as long as you do not try to charge a frozen battery.

3. Do Not Drain to Zero

Unlike NiCd batteries, lithium-ion batteries should not be fully drained. The BMS in your battery will shut off the tool before the cells reach a dangerously low voltage, but repeatedly running batteries until the tool stops is harder on the cells than stopping with some charge remaining. If you notice the tool slowing down, swap the battery.

4. Let Hot Batteries Cool Before Charging

After heavy use (running a circular saw or grinder), the battery will be warm or even hot. Let it cool to room temperature before placing it on the charger. Charging a hot battery forces more chemical stress on the cells and reduces overall lifespan. Most quality chargers have a "cooling delay" feature that waits for the battery to cool, but not all do.

5. Use the Correct Charger

Always use the charger designed for your battery platform. While some third-party chargers work fine, cheap knockoff chargers may lack proper BMS communication and can overcharge cells. If you want a faster charger, buy the manufacturer's rapid charger rather than a third-party unit.

6. Keep Contacts Clean

Dirty or corroded battery contacts create resistance, which generates heat and reduces performance. Wipe battery terminals with a dry cloth periodically, and use electrical contact cleaner if you notice any corrosion. A thin film of dielectric grease can help prevent future corrosion in humid environments.

Pro Tip

Buy at least two batteries for every tool platform you use. Rotating between batteries reduces the number of charge cycles on each one, and you will always have a charged battery ready when one runs out. The cost of a second battery pays for itself in extended battery lifespan and zero downtime.

Best Battery Platforms Compared

Choosing a cordless tool platform is one of the biggest decisions a tool buyer makes because once you invest in batteries and chargers, switching brands means starting over. Here is an honest comparison of the four biggest platforms available in 2026.

DeWalt 20V MAX

DeWalt's 20V MAX platform is the most popular professional cordless system in North America, with over 300 tools in the lineup. The batteries are widely available at every major retailer, and DeWalt frequently runs promotions where you get free batteries with tool purchases. The FLEXVOLT system adds backward-compatible 60V capability for heavy-duty tools. Battery quality is excellent, and the 20V MAX chargers are fast and reliable. The main downside is price: DeWalt batteries are among the most expensive per amp-hour.

Milwaukee M18

Milwaukee's M18 platform is the other dominant professional system, with over 280 tools. Milwaukee has been particularly aggressive about expanding into specialty trades (plumbing, electrical, HVAC) with purpose-built tools that other brands do not offer. Their HIGH OUTPUT batteries use an improved cell arrangement that delivers more power to demanding tools. Milwaukee batteries tend to be slightly heavier than equivalent DeWalt packs but are built to take a beating. The M18 charger lineup includes the Super Charger, which can fully charge a 5.0Ah pack in about 45 minutes.

Makita 18V LXT

Makita was the first major brand to go all-in on lithium-ion technology (launching their 18V LXT platform in 2005), and they have more experience with Li-Ion battery design than anyone. Their platform includes over 325 tools, making it the largest single-voltage system available. Makita batteries are known for excellent build quality and longevity. Their BL series batteries with Star Protection technology communicate with the tool in real time to protect against overloading, over-discharging, and overheating. For users who want the best battery technology specifically, Makita is hard to beat.

Ryobi ONE+ 18V

Ryobi's ONE+ platform is the value champion, offering over 330 tools (including a huge range of outdoor power equipment and lifestyle products) at prices that are typically 30-50% lower than the professional brands. The ONE+ system has maintained backward compatibility since 1996, meaning batteries purchased today work with tools from nearly three decades ago. Battery quality is good but not exceptional; you will notice slightly shorter lifespans compared to DeWalt or Milwaukee packs. The tradeoff is that Ryobi batteries cost significantly less to replace. For homeowners and DIYers who need a broad range of tools without breaking the bank, Ryobi is the clear winner on value.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I use a higher Ah battery than the one that came with my tool?

Yes, absolutely. A higher Ah battery will give you longer runtime without harming the tool. The tool draws only the current it needs regardless of the battery's Ah rating. The only tradeoff is the extra weight and size of a higher-capacity battery.

Are third-party batteries safe to use?

This is a controversial topic. Some third-party batteries work fine and cost significantly less. However, they typically lack the sophisticated BMS communication that brand-name batteries provide, and quality control varies wildly. If a $15 battery damages a $300 tool, you have saved nothing. My recommendation is to stick with genuine batteries and watch for sales, particularly the buy-a-tool-get-a-free-battery promotions that all major brands run regularly.

How long should a cordless tool battery last?

A quality lithium-ion battery should last 800-1,000 charge cycles, which translates to roughly 3-5 years of typical homeowner use or 1-3 years of daily professional use. If your battery is losing noticeable capacity before the 2-year mark, something is wrong, either a defective pack or improper storage and charging habits.

Should I leave batteries on the charger?

Modern smart chargers switch to a maintenance or trickle mode when the battery is full, so leaving a battery on the charger for a few days is generally fine. However, leaving batteries on the charger for weeks or months is not ideal. For long-term storage, remove the battery and store it at 40-60% charge in a cool, dry location.

Is it worth switching from Ryobi to DeWalt or Milwaukee?

It depends on your needs. If your Ryobi tools handle your projects without issue, there is no reason to switch. You would be spending hundreds of dollars on new batteries and chargers for incremental performance gains. Switching makes sense if you are doing professional-level work where the extra durability and power of pro-grade tools justifies the investment, or if you are starting fresh and prefer to invest in a professional platform from the beginning.

Can I use batteries from one brand in another brand's tools?

No, not without an adapter. Battery platforms are proprietary, and each brand uses a different physical connection and electronic communication protocol. Adapters do exist (for example, to use DeWalt batteries in Milwaukee tools), but they add bulk, can void warranties, and may not pass through all BMS data correctly. I do not recommend them.
