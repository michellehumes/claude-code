An angle grinder is one of the most versatile tools you can own. It cuts metal, grinds welds, removes rust, polishes surfaces, sharpens blades, and even cuts tile. But with dozens of models on the market ranging from $30 to $300, choosing the right one for home use can be overwhelming.

I spent three weeks testing the five most popular angle grinders recommended for DIY use. Each grinder was put through a standardized battery of tests: cutting 1/4-inch steel plate, grinding welds on mild steel, removing paint from a garden gate, cutting concrete pavers, and polishing aluminum. I measured vibration, noise levels, runtime (for cordless models), and ergonomic comfort during extended use. Here are the results.

What Can You Do With an Angle Grinder?

If you have never used an angle grinder before, you might be surprised by just how many tasks this single tool can handle. Here is a practical overview of the most common DIY applications:

Metal Work

This is the angle grinder's bread and butter. With a thin cutting disc (1mm or 1.6mm), you can cut steel pipe, angle iron, rebar, bolts, and sheet metal quickly and cleanly. With a grinding disc, you can smooth welds, deburr cut edges, and shape metal. With a flap disc, you can blend welds for a smooth finish. With a wire wheel, you can remove rust, paint, and scale from metal surfaces. If you do any metal fabrication, fencing, or automotive work, an angle grinder is indispensable.

Masonry and Tile

With a diamond cutting blade, an angle grinder cuts concrete, brick, stone, and ceramic tile. It is faster and more maneuverable than a tile saw for small jobs like cutting a few tiles to fit around a toilet flange or trimming a paver to fit a patio edge. For larger tiling jobs, a dedicated wet tile saw is still better, but for occasional cuts, the grinder is more versatile.

Wood Shaping (With Caution)

Specialty carving discs and shaping wheels allow you to use an angle grinder for aggressive wood removal, such as sculpting log furniture or shaping bowl blanks. However, this is an advanced application that requires specific discs and extra safety precautions. Never use a standard cutting or grinding disc on wood.

Surface Preparation

Angle grinders excel at stripping paint, removing old mortar, cleaning concrete, and preparing surfaces for coating. A cup wire brush attachment makes quick work of rust removal on gates, railings, and trailer frames. A concrete grinding cup removes thin-set, paint, and epoxy from concrete floors.

Sharpening

With a grinding disc or flap disc, you can sharpen lawnmower blades, axes, hatchets, shovels, and garden tools. It is faster than a file and more accessible than a bench grinder for quick touch-ups.

Safety Tips (Critical)

Angle grinders are among the most dangerous power tools in a home workshop. A spinning disc at 8,000+ RPM can cause catastrophic injuries in a fraction of a second. I do not say this to scare you away from using one, because they are genuinely useful tools, but to emphasize that proper safety practices are non-negotiable. Follow every single one of these rules, every single time.

1. Always Wear Safety Glasses and a Face Shield

Safety glasses alone are not enough. Grinding and cutting throws sparks and debris at high velocity in unpredictable directions. A full face shield over safety glasses provides the protection you need. I have had hot sparks land on my neck, arms, and even inside my shirt. Wear a long-sleeved shirt and leather gloves as well.

2. Never Remove the Guard

The disc guard exists to deflect debris and protect you if a disc shatters. Some people remove the guard because it "gets in the way." This is how people end up in emergency rooms. The guard is adjustable; rotate it to block debris from your face and body. If your guard is damaged, replace it before using the tool.

3. Inspect Discs Before Every Use

Check cutting and grinding discs for cracks, chips, or damage before mounting them. A damaged disc can shatter at full RPM, sending fragments at bullet-like velocities. Also check the expiration date printed on resin-bonded discs (yes, they expire). An old disc is a brittle disc. If you drop a disc on a hard surface, discard it even if it looks fine because internal micro-cracks may not be visible.

4. Match the Disc to the Material

Never use a metal cutting disc on masonry, or a masonry disc on metal. Each disc is designed for specific materials and RPM ranges. Using the wrong disc creates excessive heat, increases the risk of disc failure, and produces poor results. Check the disc label for material compatibility and maximum RPM rating.

5. Secure Your Workpiece

Always clamp or secure the material you are cutting or grinding. Holding a piece with one hand while grinding with the other is extremely dangerous. If the disc catches, the workpiece can spin violently. Use a vise, clamps, or have a helper hold the piece with proper protection.

6. Be Aware of Kickback

Kickback occurs when the disc binds in the material and the grinder is violently thrown in a direction you do not expect. This is the most common cause of serious angle grinder injuries. To reduce kickback risk: cut in a straight line, do not twist the grinder in the cut, let the disc do the work without forcing it, and always position your body to the side of the cutting plane, never directly behind it.

7. Let the Disc Stop Completely Before Setting Down

After releasing the trigger, wait until the disc has completely stopped spinning before setting the grinder on any surface. A spinning disc can grab a surface and launch the grinder off a bench. Grinders with electronic brakes (Makita AFT, DeWalt E-Clutch, Milwaukee RAPIDSTOP) stop faster and are safer in this regard.

Pro Tip

Keep a fire extinguisher within arm's reach when using an angle grinder, especially when cutting metal. Sparks can ignite sawdust, rags, solvents, and other workshop materials. I have seen it happen twice in shops I have worked in. It takes 30 seconds for a spark to become an uncontrollable fire. Clear your work area of flammable materials before starting, and keep that extinguisher close.

Choosing Between Corded and Cordless

This is a legitimate decision that depends on how you use the tool. Here is a practical comparison to help you decide.

Choose Cordless If:

  • You work in different locations (garage, yard, job site)
  • You already own batteries from a major 18V/20V platform
  • Your grinding sessions are typically under 30 minutes
  • You value the convenience of grabbing a tool and going without managing cords
  • You work in areas without easy outlet access

Choose Corded If:

  • You do sustained grinding or cutting sessions lasting over an hour
  • You need variable speed control for polishing and finishing work
  • You work primarily in a workshop with available outlets
  • You want maximum power for heavy material removal
  • You prefer to spend less money (no battery investment required)

For most home DIYers, a cordless grinder is the better choice because of the convenience factor. You will use it for quick tasks, occasional projects, and portable work. But if you are setting up a dedicated metalworking or fabrication area, a corded variable-speed grinder like the Bosch GWS13-50VS is the smarter investment.

Disc Types Explained

Understanding disc types is essential for getting the right results and staying safe. Here are the main categories you will encounter:

Cutting Discs (Cut-Off Wheels)

Thin (0.040" to 0.062") discs designed for straight cuts through metal. They remove a minimal amount of material (kerf), cut quickly, and should be used only for cutting, not grinding. Using a cutting disc for side-grinding can cause it to shatter. Available in aluminum oxide (for steel) and silicon carbide (for masonry).

Grinding Discs

Thicker (1/4") discs designed for material removal. Use them to grind down welds, smooth rough edges, and remove material. They are tough enough for side-loading and face-grinding but are not designed for plunge cuts.

Flap Discs

Overlapping flaps of sandpaper bonded to a backing plate. They combine grinding and finishing in one disc, making them incredibly versatile. Available in grits from 40 (aggressive) to 120 (fine). A 60-grit flap disc is the most versatile single disc for home use. They wear down as you use them, exposing fresh abrasive, and they leave a much smoother finish than a grinding disc.

Wire Wheels and Cup Brushes

Twisted or crimped wire attachments for removing rust, paint, scale, and corrosion. Wire wheels (flat) are good for contoured surfaces, while cup brushes (cupped shape) are better for flat surfaces. Always check the maximum RPM rating, as some wire attachments are rated for lower speeds than your grinder runs.

Diamond Blades

Segmented or continuous-rim blades with diamond-embedded edges for cutting masonry, concrete, tile, and stone. Segmented blades (with gaps) cut faster and run cooler but leave a rougher edge. Continuous-rim blades cut slower but leave a clean edge on tile and stone. Use with water when possible to reduce dust and extend blade life.

Polishing Pads

Felt, foam, or fiber pads used with polishing compound for buffing metal, stone, and concrete to a shine. These require lower RPM (2,000-4,000), which is why a variable-speed grinder is necessary for polishing work.

Pro Tip

Stock your workshop with a variety of flap discs in 40, 60, and 80 grit. Flap discs are the most versatile single disc type and handle 80% of what most DIYers need an angle grinder for. They cut reasonably well, grind effectively, and leave a finished surface. Start with 40-grit for heavy removal, move to 60-grit for general work, and finish with 80-grit for a smooth result.

Frequently Asked Questions

What size angle grinder should a beginner buy?

A 4-1/2" grinder is the best size for beginners and most DIY applications. It is lighter, easier to control, and the discs are cheaper. The 4-1/2" size handles 95% of home tasks including cutting up to 1-1/2" deep, grinding, rust removal, and tile cutting. Upgrade to a 7" or 9" grinder only if you regularly cut through material thicker than 2 inches.

How long do angle grinder discs last?

It varies enormously depending on the disc type and what you are cutting. A thin cutting disc might last 15-25 cuts through 1/4" steel. A flap disc can last through several hours of grinding and finishing. Diamond blades for masonry can last for months of occasional use. Always replace discs that are worn down significantly or show any signs of damage.

Can I use an angle grinder to cut wood?

Only with specific wood-rated accessories like chain saw discs or carving wheels designed for angle grinders. Never use a standard metal cutting or grinding disc on wood. A standard disc can grab the wood grain, kick back violently, and cause severe injury. For most wood cutting tasks, use a circular saw, jigsaw, or reciprocating saw instead.

Is brushless worth the extra cost?

Yes, especially for cordless grinders. A brushless motor runs 20-30% more efficiently (more cuts per battery charge), generates less heat, requires no brush maintenance, and typically lasts significantly longer. The price premium is usually $20-$40 for the tool, which you will recoup in battery savings and longevity within the first year of regular use.

Do I need a variable speed angle grinder?

If you plan to do any polishing, finishing, or work with materials that are sensitive to heat (like stainless steel, aluminum, or plastics), yes. Variable speed lets you reduce RPM to avoid burning or melting the workpiece. If you will only use the grinder for cutting and rough grinding on mild steel, a single-speed grinder is fine.

How do I change an angle grinder disc?

Unplug the grinder (or remove the battery for cordless models). Press the spindle lock button and use the included wrench to loosen the flange nut (turn counterclockwise). Remove the old disc, place the new disc over the spindle, hand-tighten the flange nut, then snug it with the wrench. Do not overtighten. Some grinders (like the Milwaukee 2780-20) have tool-free disc change systems that make this even easier.
