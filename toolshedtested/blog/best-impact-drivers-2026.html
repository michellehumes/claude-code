An impact driver looks like a drill. It fits the same charger. It comes in the same kit box. But it does something a drill cannot: it delivers violent rotational hammering bursts on top of continuous rotation, giving it torque that is three to five times higher than a standard drill driver. The result is that screws that would strip out, cam out, or require two hands on a drill drive home effortlessly with an impact driver.

If you drive a lot of screws, lag bolts, or structural fasteners, an impact driver will change how you work. Based on our testing across five current models, here are the best options for 2026.

The DeWalt DCF887B is the best alternative for anyone already in the DeWalt ecosystem. On a budget, the Ryobi PCF11K ONE+ HP brings brushless performance at a fraction of the price.

Why You Need an Impact Driver (Not Just a Drill)

The most common question we get: "I already have a drill. Do I really need an impact driver?" The answer depends on what you build. If you drive screws for more than 20 minutes a month, yes.

A drill applies continuous torque. When that torque exceeds the friction holding the screw, it stalls, strips the head, or twists your wrist. An impact driver solves this by adding up to 4,000 concussive impacts per minute alongside the rotation. Each impact delivers a burst of angular force that keeps the screw moving without stalling. You can drive a 3-inch deck screw into Douglas fir with one hand. Lag bolts that require two hands and full body weight on a drill go in with a flick of the trigger.

The tradeoff is precision control. The impacts make it harder to sink a screw to an exact depth or drive at a specific torque. For delicate applications where you need fine clutch control, a drill is still the right tool. Pros own both. For most DIYers, if budget forces a choice, get the impact driver first.

What to Look for When Buying an Impact Driver

Torque Rating

For most DIY tasks including decking, furniture assembly, and general construction, 1,200 to 1,500 in-lb is sufficient. For driving 3-inch structural screws, lag bolts, and repeated heavy fastening, look for 1,700 in-lb or more. The Milwaukee at 2,000 in-lb and the DeWalt models at 1,825 in-lb cover virtually any situation a homeowner will encounter.

Brushless vs. Brushed Motor

In 2026, there is essentially no reason to buy a brushed impact driver at the $100-plus price point. Brushless motors run 20 to 40 percent more efficiently (more drives per charge), generate less heat, and last significantly longer. Every model in this roundup uses a brushless motor. If you find a budget impact driver with a brushed motor, treat it as a red flag unless you are buying it as a throwaway tool for a single project.

Multi-Speed Modes

Single-speed impact drivers are fine for general construction, but multi-speed models give you genuine versatility. A low-speed mode allows you to drive small screws into thin trim, plywood, and MDF without blowing through the material. A high-speed mode maximizes throughput on structural work. If you work with varied materials, the three-speed DeWalt or four-mode Milwaukee is worth the premium.

Battery Ecosystem

The most important factor most buyers overlook: battery platform. Once you invest in two or three batteries for a platform, switching brands becomes expensive. Milwaukee M18 and DeWalt 20V MAX are the two most versatile platforms, with the most tool options and the widest battery selection. Makita LXT is excellent for users already in their ecosystem. Ryobi ONE+ is the value platform. If you are starting fresh, pick Milwaukee or DeWalt and stick with it.

Head Length

Head length determines how well the driver fits in tight spaces. Under 5 inches is compact; 5 to 5.5 inches is standard; over 6 inches is bulky. For cabinet work and confined spaces, the DeWalt ATOMIC at 3.97 inches has a meaningful advantage. For general use, 5 to 5.5 inches is fine.

Pro Tip

Buy a set of impact-rated bits before you start driving screws. Standard drill bits are not hardened for the concussive impact force generated by impact drivers and will snap or cam out. Look for sets marked "impact rated" or "impact ready." The Makita ImpactX and DeWalt IMPACT READY lines are both well-regarded and reasonably priced.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the difference between an impact driver and a drill?

A drill uses continuous rotational force and is ideal for drilling holes and light screw driving with fine clutch control. An impact driver adds rapid rotational impacts (up to 4,000 per minute) to the rotation, giving it far more torque for driving long screws, lag bolts, and fasteners into hard materials. Impact drivers strip heads and overpower clutch control in situations where a drill's precision is needed. Pros own both. For most DIYers choosing one, the impact driver drives screws and the drill handles precision drilling.

How much torque do I need in an impact driver?

For most DIY tasks like decking, framing, and furniture assembly, 1,200 to 1,500 in-lb is plenty. For driving 3-inch lag bolts into structural lumber or working repeatedly with hardwoods, look for 1,800 to 2,000 in-lb. Beyond 2,000 in-lb, you are in professional contractor territory and likely buying a 1/2-inch impact wrench rather than an impact driver.

Can I use an impact driver to drill holes?

Yes, with limitations. Use a 1/4-inch hex shank drill bit and the impact driver will drill holes in wood and drywall just fine. For precise drilling, countersinking, or drilling into masonry, a standard drill gives you better control. Many pros own both tools for this reason.

Is brushless worth the premium on an impact driver?

Yes, absolutely. Brushless impact drivers run cooler, last longer, and get significantly more drive cycles per battery charge. For a tool you use frequently, the brushless premium pays for itself within the first year. Every tool in this roundup is brushless for this reason.

What hex shank size do impact drivers use?

The vast majority of cordless impact drivers use a 1/4-inch hex chuck. This is the universal standard for impact driver bits, nut drivers, and quick-change sockets. All of the impact drivers in this roundup use 1/4-inch hex chucks.
