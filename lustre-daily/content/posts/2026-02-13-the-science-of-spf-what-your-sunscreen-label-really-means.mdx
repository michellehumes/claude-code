Sunscreen is the single most impactful product in any skincare routine, yet it's also the most misunderstood. Marketing claims and confusing labels make it hard to know what you're actually getting.

Let's break down the science so you can make informed choices about sun protection.

## What SPF Actually Measures

SPF stands for Sun Protection Factor, but it doesn't measure total protection — it measures how much longer you can stay in the sun before UVB rays cause redness compared to unprotected skin.

- **SPF 15** blocks about 93% of UVB rays
- **SPF 30** blocks about 97% of UVB rays
- **SPF 50** blocks about 98% of UVB rays
- **SPF 100** blocks about 99% of UVB rays

The jump from SPF 30 to SPF 50 is only a 1% difference in UVB blocking. This is why dermatologists generally recommend SPF 30-50 as the sweet spot.

<Callout type="info">
SPF only measures UVB protection (the rays that cause sunburn). For UVA protection (the rays that cause aging and deeper damage), look for "broad spectrum" on the label.
</Callout>

## Chemical vs. Mineral Sunscreen

### Chemical Sunscreens
These absorb UV rays and convert them to heat. Common filters include avobenzone, octinoxate, and oxybenzone.

**Pros**: Lightweight, no white cast, cosmetically elegant
**Cons**: Can irritate sensitive skin, some environmental concerns, need 15-20 minutes to activate

### Mineral Sunscreens
These sit on top of skin and physically block UV rays. The active ingredients are zinc oxide and/or titanium dioxide.

**Pros**: Immediate protection, gentle on sensitive skin, reef-friendly
**Cons**: Can leave white cast, often thicker texture, may feel heavier

### Hybrid Sunscreens
Many modern formulas combine both types for the benefits of each. These are increasingly popular and well-tolerated.

## Decoding Label Claims

### "Water-Resistant"
By FDA regulation, this means the SPF value has been tested after 40 minutes (water-resistant) or 80 minutes (very water-resistant) of water exposure. No sunscreen is waterproof — that term is no longer allowed on labels.

### "Broad Spectrum"
This means the product protects against both UVA and UVB rays. UVA rays penetrate deeper into skin and are the primary cause of premature aging. Always choose broad spectrum.

### "Reef-Safe" or "Reef-Friendly"
There's no FDA regulation for these terms. Generally, they mean the formula excludes oxybenzone and octinoxate, which have shown potential harm to coral reefs. Mineral-only formulas are the safest bet for ocean use.

### "Non-Comedogenic"
This means the formula has been tested not to clog pores. However, everyone's skin is different, so this isn't a guarantee.

<Callout type="expert" title="Expert Perspective">
The best sunscreen is the one you'll actually wear every day. An SPF 30 you apply generously and reapply beats an SPF 50 you use sparingly.
</Callout>

## How Much Sunscreen Do You Actually Need?

Most people apply far too little sunscreen:

- **Face and neck**: About 1/4 teaspoon (a nickel-sized amount)
- **Each arm**: About 1/2 teaspoon
- **Full body**: About 1 ounce (a shot glass)
- **Reapplication**: Every 2 hours, or after swimming/sweating

If you're using SPF 30 but only applying half the tested amount, you're getting roughly SPF 7 protection. Application quantity matters more than SPF number.

## Sunscreen Under Makeup

For those who wear makeup daily:

1. Apply sunscreen as the last step of skincare
2. Wait 2-3 minutes for it to set before primer or foundation
3. Choose a sunscreen with a matte or silicone-like finish for best makeup compatibility
4. Reapply over makeup using a sunscreen spray or powder SPF

## Common Myths Debunked

**"Dark skin doesn't need sunscreen"** — False. While melanin provides some natural protection, all skin tones are susceptible to UV damage, hyperpigmentation, and skin cancer.

**"You can't get sunburned on cloudy days"** — False. Up to 80% of UV rays penetrate clouds.

**"SPF in makeup is enough"** — Unlikely. You'd need to apply 7-14 layers of foundation to get the labeled SPF value.

**"Sunscreen causes vitamin D deficiency"** — Studies show that regular sunscreen users don't have significantly lower vitamin D levels, likely because no one applies perfectly or consistently enough to block all UV.

## Final Thoughts

Sunscreen doesn't need to be complicated. Choose a broad-spectrum SPF 30-50 that you enjoy wearing, apply enough of it, and reapply throughout the day. That simple habit will do more for your skin's long-term health and appearance than any other product in your routine.
